# Interview Prep — Langstaff's 3-Phase System + 101 Questions Framework

## The Core Insight

Most candidates prep by memorizing answers. Langstaff's system flips it: **predict the questions first**, then map your stories to them. This approach means you're never surprised and you walk in knowing which career moment you'll pull for every question.

---

## Phase 1: Dissect the Job Posting

Read the job posting and highlight every:
- **Job duty or task** ("manage a team of 10")
- **Required skill** ("proficiency in Salesforce")
- **Industry terminology** ("ARR," "churn reduction," "GTM strategy")
- **Technology or tool** ("Excel," "Jira," "Python")
- **Professional trait** ("collaborative," "data-driven," "self-starter")

---

## Phase 2: Convert Keywords to Questions

Take every highlighted item and convert it to a behavioral question:

| Keyword | Predicted Question |
|---------|-------------------|
| "manage a team" | "Tell me about a time you had to manage a difficult team member." |
| "Salesforce" | "Walk me through how you've used Salesforce to manage a pipeline." |
| "data-driven" | "Describe a situation where data changed a decision you were about to make." |
| "fast-paced environment" | "Tell me about a time you had to manage multiple competing priorities." |

**Target:** 15–30 predicted questions before any interview. More is better.

---

## Phase 3: Map Your Career Stories

For each predicted question, note:
- Which career story / accomplishment you'll use
- Which formula you'll use to tell it (STAR or CARL)

After doing this across 20+ questions, you'll notice the same 5–8 stories cover most of the questions — that's fine and expected. The prep isn't about memorizing 20 different answers; it's about knowing your 6–8 best stories cold.

---

## The STAR Answer Structure

**Best for:** All behavioral questions ("Tell me about a time...")

| Component | What to Cover |
|-----------|--------------|
| **S**ituation | Set the scene — brief context (1–3 sentences) |
| **T**ask | What was your specific responsibility in that situation |
| **A**ction | What YOU specifically did — use "I," not "we" |
| **R**esult | What happened because of your actions — with numbers if possible |

**Timing:** 1–3 minutes per answer. Practice out loud — don't just rehearse in your head.

**After your answer:** Circle back with a brief tie to the role: "That experience is part of why this position interests me — I'd love to bring that same approach here."

---

## The CARL Answer Structure

**Best for:** When you want to show growth mindset (especially for Director+ roles)

| Component | What to Cover |
|-----------|--------------|
| **C**ontext | Brief background |
| **A**ction | What you did |
| **R**esult | What happened |
| **L**earning | What you took away and how you applied it since |

The Learning element is powerful for roles that involve continuous improvement, managing change, or leadership development.

## The "Why Are You Interested?" 3-Part Answer

One of the most common questions — and most blown by candidates who give vague answers.

**Three-part structure:**
1. **Responsibilities you're excited about** — specific tasks from the job posting you're looking forward to doing
2. **Why this company specifically** — research-based, not generic ("I love your products")
3. **Why this industry / field** — when/why you became interested, what keeps you engaged

**Example:**
> "There are a few things that drew me to this role. First, the opportunity to build the analytics function from scratch is exactly the kind of challenge I thrive in — I did something similar at my last company and it was the most rewarding work I've done. On the company side, I've been following your Series B growth and the direction you're taking the platform for mid-market clients — that's a segment I have deep experience with. And honestly, I've been in B2B SaaS analytics for 8 years now and it's where my skills are sharpest. This role felt like a natural next step."

## The 10 Interview No-No's (Langstaff's List)

| # | Never Do This | Why |
|---|--------------|-----|
| 1 | Ask about salary or benefits during the interview | Ask only after receiving an offer, before accepting |
| 2 | Say anything negative about a former employer or manager | Makes you look difficult, not them |
| 3 | Say "When will I hear back?" | Sounds desperate and presumptuous |
| 4 | Swear | Even casually — unprofessional in most settings |
| 5 | Say "I don't have any questions for you" | Always have 3–5 questions prepared |
| 6 | Trail off without finishing a thought ("And... uh... yeah") | Either circle back and finish, or stop and smile |
| 7 | Say "I'm nervous" | Replace with: "I'm excited" — same energy, positive framing |
| 8 | Ask about schedule flexibility, WFH, or sick days | Save for after the offer |
| 9 | Say "It's in my resume" if asked about something on it | They want to hear you talk about it — explain it |
| 10 | Come without researching company, industry, and interviewers | Non-negotiable preparation step |

## Questions to Ask the Interviewer

**Never leave an interview without asking 3–5 thoughtful questions.** Categories:

**About the role:**
- "What does success look like in this role at 90 days? At one year?"
- "What are the biggest challenges the person in this role will need to solve first?"
- "What does a typical week look like for this position?"

**About the team:**
- "How would you describe the team culture and how the team likes to work?"
- "What do the most successful people on this team have in common?"

**About the company:**
- "What are the company's top priorities over the next 12 months?"
- "What do you find most exciting about where the company is headed?"

**About the interviewer:**
- "What's been your favorite part of working here?"
- "What made you join this company?"

**Signal interest:**
- "Based on our conversation, is there anything about my background that gives you pause? I'd love the chance to address it."

## Pre-Interview Research Checklist

Before any interview, know:

- [ ] Company: what they do, who they serve, recent news, funding, leadership
- [ ] Industry: key trends, competitive landscape, major challenges
- [ ] Interviewers: LinkedIn profile, recent posts, shared connections
- [ ] Role: re-read JD the morning of; have predicted questions fresh
- [ ] Your stories: review your top 6–8 career stories out loud
- [ ] Logistics: location, parking, virtual link, who to contact if running late

## Post-Interview: The Thank You Email

Send within 24 hours (same day is better).

**Structure:**
1. Thank them for the time
2. Reference one specific thing from the conversation (shows you were present)
3. Reiterate your enthusiasm for the role and why you're a strong fit
4. Express you look forward to next steps

**Length:** 3–5 sentences. Not an essay — a genuine, professional note.
