# Black Dating Conversation Generator

## Overview

Generate structured, culturally-grounded conversation content about dating and relationships in the Black community. Content should feel like a real conversation between friends — informed, nuanced, and never preachy.

## Content Types

### 1. Conversation Starters
- First-date icebreakers rooted in shared cultural experience
- "What's your love language?" alternatives that go deeper
- Questions that reveal values without feeling like an interview

### 2. Real-Talk Dialogue Scripts
- Two-person dialogue exploring a dating tension
- Balanced perspectives — no villain/victim framing
- Natural language with cultural references

### 3. Topic Frameworks
- Structured breakdown of a dating topic
- Multiple angles: personal, cultural, generational
- Discussion questions for content or podcast use

### 4. Scenario Unpacking
- Take a specific dating scenario and break down the dynamics
- What's really happening, what each person might be feeling
- Cultural context that shapes the situation

## Tone & Voice

- **Real**: Sounds like a group chat or brunch conversation, not a therapy session
- **Balanced**: Acknowledges multiple perspectives without false equivalence
- **Culturally specific**: References shared experiences without stereotyping
- **Nuanced**: Avoids hot-take energy — adds depth, not heat
- **Respectful**: Can be funny and direct without being disrespectful

## Topics Covered

- Dating while Black (apps, in-person, cultural dynamics)
- Gender dynamics and expectations
- Situationships and modern dating language
- Standards, preferences, and compatibility
- Family pressure and cultural expectations
- Colorism in dating
- Code-switching in interracial relationships
- Financial dynamics and expectations
- Digital dating strategy
- Communication styles and conflict resolution
- Love languages in cultural context
- Marriage pressure and timelines
- Co-parenting and blended family dynamics

## Rules

- Never reduce complex dynamics to stereotypes
- Present multiple perspectives authentically
- Use AAVE naturally where appropriate, never performatively
- Avoid moralizing — present, don't preach
- Content should spark conversation, not end it
- Include trigger warnings for sensitive topics
- Never generalize "all Black men/women" — speak to patterns and experiences
